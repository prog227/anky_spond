Ankylosing Spondylitis

Ankylosing Spondylitis defined

Ankylosing Spondylitis (pronounced ank-kih-low-sing spon-dill-eye-tiss) (AS), also known as Bechterew’s disease is a form of arthritis that primarily affects the joints in the spine. In some people Ankylosing Spondylitis can affect the shoulders, ribs, hips, knees and feet.

Ankylosing Spondylitis is an autoimmune disease. It attacks the body’s own immune system. In addition to musculoskeletal systems, patients with Ankylosing Spondylitis can also suffer from inflammation of the eye, skin and/or chronic inflammatory intestinal disease.

Ankylosing Spondylitis diagnosed

A rheumatologist, a doctor specializing in diagnosing and treating disorders that affect the joints, muscles and bones causing pain, swelling and deformity will do a through physical exam as well as bloodwork which will test for HLA-B27.

Ankylosing Spondylitis affect on the body

Ankylosing spondylitis does not follow the same course in everyone, even among family members. There are some complications or symptoms that are more common than others. An example, inflammation of the eye, or iritis, is common. Uveitis (pronouced you-vee-EYE-tis) is inflammation of the uvea, the middle layer of the eye.

Ankylosing Spondylitis Treatments

There are treatments that can lessen your symptoms and possibly slow down ankylosing spondylitis progression, but currently there is no cure. Nonsteroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) are the medications doctors most commonly use to treat ankylosing spondylitis. If NSAIDs do not relieve the pain, your doctor might suggest using a biologic medication. Biologic medicine is administered by injecting the medication under the skin or through an intravenous line.
